Verses 9-16



Genuine Love for One Another

In Romans 12:9, Paul starts with a simple yet profound statement: "Let love be genuine." What does that mean? It’s about loving people without pretending. Sometimes, we can feel pressure to be nice on the surface, but inside, we’re frustrated or judging. Genuine love, though, is pure—it’s the type of love that comes from the heart and is fueled by kindness, honesty, and grace. It’s about seeing your fellow believers as brothers and sisters in Christ and wanting the best for them, even when it’s hard.

Hate Evil, Cling to Good

Paul continues with the idea that we should “hate what is evil; cling to what is good” (v.9). This means being intentional about rejecting things that go against God’s ways while choosing what is good and true. In our relationships with other Christians, we have to be careful about gossip, selfishness, or envy creeping in. Instead, we need to focus on supporting, encouraging, and lifting each other up.

Serving with Zeal

In verse 11, Paul encourages us not to be lazy but to “serve the Lord with zeal.” In the body of Christ, everyone has a role to play. Whether you’re helping out at church, leading a Bible study, or just being there for a friend, God wants you to do it with excitement and passion. When we’re all working together, using our unique gifts, the church becomes a place of joy and growth.

Rejoicing and Weeping Together

One of the most beautiful parts of being in the body of Christ is that we don’t have to go through life alone. Paul tells us to “rejoice with those who rejoice and weep with those who weep” (v.15). This means celebrating the wins of our friends, not out of jealousy, but out of genuine happiness for them. And when one of us is hurting, we come alongside, offering comfort and understanding.

Living in Harmony

Lastly, Paul calls us to “live in harmony with one another” (v.16). This means setting aside pride, grudges, or judgment and instead working together in unity. Harmony doesn’t mean we always agree, but it means we respect, love, and build each other up, focused on God’s mission for His people.

Matt’s decision to forgive Travis for ruining his soccer game and turning their friends against him exemplifies the genuine love described in Romans 12:9-16. Instead of holding a grudge, Matt chose forgiveness, embodying the call to "let love be genuine" (v.9). This choice reflects the principles of hating what is evil and clinging to what is good (v.9), as Matt focused on kindness rather than resentment. By forgiving Travis, he demonstrated the harmony Paul encourages in verse 16, setting aside pride and working towards unity.
While their situation seems simple, the emotions—betrayal, hurt, and the struggle to forgive—are universal. Whether it’s a disagreement with a friend or a deeper wound, the call to forgive and show love applies just as much in our own lives.
